On Caring for the Mind

From: The Stoic Manual Apr 26, 2026 Label: Philosophy AI Summary 
Opening with Marcus Aurelius ('of body, breath, and mind, the third alone is truly yours'), the essay argues most people leave their most important possession unattended. Three practices for tending the mind: (1) Control your inputs — the mind absorbs its surroundings like a sponge; Epictetus called this 'prosoche' (attention, vigilance); avoid social media first thing; fill the day with books, substantive conversations, and silence instead; 'The soul becomes dyed the color of your thoughts' (Marcus Aurelius); (2) Refuse to sell yourself cheaply — stop rewriting messages out of fear of judgment; stop checking who viewed your story; stop bartering small pieces of peace for others' opinions; let others have their flimsy opinions and keep your composure; Marcus retreated not to villas but to himself: 'No retreat offers someone more quiet than that into his own mind'; (3) Direct attention toward what's yours to do — exercise (sluggish body drags the mind down), sleep, read, write, meditate, sit in silence long enough to generate a genuine thought. The reward: a mind that steers you through fear before difficult conversations, earns your living, sees through manipulation, and finds the right next step. 'You start to feel lively in a way you had nearly forgotten was possible.'
